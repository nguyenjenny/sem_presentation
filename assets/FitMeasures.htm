(A)GFI (Adjusted) Goodness of Fit GFI is the proportion of variance accounted for by the estimated population covariance. Analogous to R2. AGFI favors parsimony. GFI ≥ 0.95
AGFI ≥0.90
CFI Comparative Fit Index A revised form of NFI. Not very sensitive to sample size. Compares the fit of a target model to the fit of an independent, or null, model. If the CFI is less than one, then the CFI is always greater than the TLI.  CFI pays a penalty of one for every parameter estimated.  Because the TLI and CFI are highly correlated, only one of the two should be reported.  The CFI is reported more often than the TLI, but I think the CFI’s penalty for complexity of just 1 is too low and so I prefer the TLI even though the CFI is reported much more frequently than the TLI.  CFI ≥.90

Tucker Lewis Index		 An NFI of .95, indicates the model of interest improves the fit by 95% relative to the null model. NNFI is preferable for smaller samples. Sometimes the NNFI is called the Tucker Lewis index (TLI) NFI ≥ 0.95
TLI/NNFI ≥ 0.95
RMSEA Root Mean Square Error of
Approximation		 A parsimony-adjusted index.  Values closer to
0 represent a good fit.		 RMSEA <0.08
AIC Akaike Information Criterion The AIC is a comparative measure of fit and so it is meaningful only when two different models are estimated.  Lower values indicate a better fit and so the model with the lowest AIC is the best fitting model.  The AIC makes the researcher pay a penalty of two for every parameter that is estimated. Lower values are ideal
BIC Bayersian Information Criterion Whereas the AIC has a penalty of 2 for every parameter estimated, the BIC increases the penalty as sample size increases  Lower values are ideal
